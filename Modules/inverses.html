Module 8: Matrix Inverses

This module explores matrix inverses for \(2 \times 2\) matrices.

Suppose that \(A\) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix. If \(B\) is a \(2 \times 2\) matrix and we have \(BA=I\) where \(I=\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{purple}1 & \textcolor{red}0\\ \textcolor{blue}0 & \textcolor{purple}1 \end{bmatrix}\) then we say that \(B\) is the inverse of \(A\).

Given the matrix \(A\), how can we determine its inverse, \(B\)?

If we write \(B=\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red} {b_{11}} & \textcolor{red}{b_{12}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{b_{21}} & \textcolor{blue}{b_{22}} \end{bmatrix} \) and \(A=\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {a_{11}} & \textcolor{red}{a_{12}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{a_{21}} & \textcolor{red}{a_{22}} \end{bmatrix} \) the condition that \(BA=I\) means that

  • the row vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red} {b_{11}} & \textcolor{red}{b_{12}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \) must be perpendicular to the column vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red}{a_{12}}\\ \textcolor{red}{a_{22}} \end{bmatrix} \) and
  • the row vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {b_{21}} & \textcolor{blue}{b_{22}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \) must be perpendicular to the column vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue}{a_{11}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{a_{21}} \end{bmatrix} \).

This uniquely determines the direction (up to a sign) of the row vectors \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red} {b_{11}} & \textcolor{red}{b_{12}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \) and \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {b_{21}} & \textcolor{blue}{b_{22}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \). All that remains is to determine their magnitude (and sign), which is determined by the equations

  • \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red} {b_{11}} & \textcolor{red}{b_{12}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue}{a_{11}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{a_{21}} \end{bmatrix} =1 \) and
  • \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {b_{21}} & \textcolor{blue}{b_{22}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red}{a_{12}}\\ \textcolor{red}{a_{22}} \end{bmatrix}=1\).

In the diagram the column vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red}{a_{12}}\\ \textcolor{red}{a_{22}} \end{bmatrix} \) is shown as a solid red line and column vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue}{a_{11}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{a_{21}} \end{bmatrix} \) is shown as a solid blue line. The row vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red} {b_{11}} & \textcolor{red}{b_{12}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \) is shown in dotted red and the row vector \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {b_{21}} & \textcolor{blue}{b_{22}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \) is shown in dotted blue. Finallly, the magnitude of \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red} {b_{11}} & \textcolor{red}{b_{12}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue}{a_{11}}\\ \textcolor{blue}{a_{21}} \end{bmatrix} \) is represented by the distance that the red dot lies from the origin and the magnitude of \(\begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{blue} {b_{21}} & \textcolor{blue}{b_{22}}\\ \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} \textcolor{red}{a_{12}}\\ \textcolor{red}{a_{22}} \end{bmatrix}\) is represented by the distance that the blue dot lies from the origin.

As a result, in order for the matrix \(B\) to be the inverse of the matrix \(A\) we must have that

  • The two red vectors make an angle of 90 degrees.
  • The two blue vectors make an angle of 90 degrees.
  • The red dot lies on the black unit circle (on the same side of the circle as the solid blue vector).
  • The blue dot lies on the black unit circle (on the same side of the circle as the solid red vector).
(The conditions in parentheses are need to make sure that the dot products are \(1\) and not \(-1\).)
